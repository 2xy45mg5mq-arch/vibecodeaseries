It's 2026. And everyone's making it up.
MAX is no exception. He's got strong experience — on paper. But with business moving to the terminal, his skillset isn't really working for the tech company where he works. Or, as of today, used to work. It's not that they don't like him. They just don't think he's…that good at things. Still, they let him stay in the office until he finds another job.
FRANKIE fucking loves it. The hype beast account manager has been preaching the primacy of "agency" since GPT-3.5 and sees Max's "liberation" as the catalyst to activate growth for the both of them. Frankie wants to build and ship — if only he knew how to build anything. He convinces Max to just make his own thing. They just need to find a thing.
JAY just thinks they're morons. The cynical sales engineer with a mortgage knows what's coming, and insists Max get another "real job" before he's trapped in the permanent underclass.
TAYARISHA has a camera and Claude supermax. She's at the office shooting marketing content, but finds Max's crashout way more interesting. A mom with a real Hollywood career behind her, she traded directing for steady work. But when her remixes of Max's firing go viral, she sees an opportunity to make something real. Even if everything Max tries is completely fake. So while he's vibe-coding his career — flailing and failing to cash in on the AI gold rush — she's using him to vibe code a series.
GOING LONG ON SHORT-FORM COMEDY
Advertising is out. Narratives are in. The market for attention is a bloodbath fought with a single weapon: story.
Someone is going to make the next tech satire — Silicon Valley for the AI boom.
Why not make it inside a tech company?
It doesn't need to be about the company. It just lives there. The office is a stage. And the brand gets cultural reach into ideal talent and client pools that compound over time, but costs less than temporary subway ads. Marketing content buys fractured attention. Creative content earns obsession.
FUNCTIONAL FORMAT
The series is multi-modal and multi-channel — scaling production as it proves its audience. Full episodes splice into vertical shorts on socials. Behind-the-scenes becomes bite-sized discovery content. Characters confide in their AI chatbots instead of the camera — confessionals for the Claude era.
Once the world is built in the office, everything that happens there — the CEO's takes, real customer stories, product demos — can exist as story color, not advertising. A post-modern soap opera. One production pipeline, infinite permutations.
STEALING THE AUDIENCE
"Vibe coding" is the most searched term in tech right now. And we can own it now. The series is itself vibe coded — built on AI-driven research pipelines that scan the news cycle for characters, controversies, and plotlines in real time. Episodes ride the coattails of subjects people are already looking for — redirecting the audience searching YouTube for AI tutorials into a story instead. Before they get stale.
Most TV series take years to make. We can make our episodes in days — staying in front of any Hollywood attempts to cash in on the cultural cachet.
THE RIGHT HOST
We're looking for a partner firm that already thinks like a media company but hasn't yet jumped from engagement to entertainment. A company that has major loyalty but minor reach outside its market. This show is a vehicle for broader capture.
The comedy punches outward — at the hype, at fake founders, at the entire fake-it-until-you-make-it vibecoding ecosystem. By operating responsibly and profitably in the background, it will be an island of competence in a sea of bullshit.
